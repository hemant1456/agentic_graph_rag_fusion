The Vertexia Story: From Stanford Labs to Data Infrastructure

It's hard to believe that Vertexia Inc. started in a cramped basement office in San Francisco back in March 2019. Looking back, it feels like a lifetime ago, yet the core mission—democratizing data engineering—remains exactly the same.

I met Diana Volkov at Stanford during our final year of grad school. We were both frustrated by the same recurring problem: data pipelines were gatekept by complex code that made it impossible for non-engineers to access the insights they needed. We spent countless nights whiteboarding, trying to figure out how to abstract away the ETL complexity without sacrificing the performance that enterprise clients demand.

We launched our first MVP with a simple hypothesis: what if we treated data flow like a visual workflow? By early 2020, we officially reached GA with NexusFlow (or "FLOW," as the internal team still calls it). It was our first real attempt at solving the data ingestion bottleneck.

The growth phase was a whirlwind. We secured our Series B in mid-2021, which gave us the fuel to scale our team and start dreaming bigger. By that time, we were already planning the expansion beyond pipelines into analytics.

Late 2021 marked a significant transition for the company. Diana, my co-founder and partner in every sense of the early journey, decided to leave Vertexia to pursue an entirely new startup venture. It was an amicable parting—she remains a valued shareholder and a close friend of the firm. Her vision laid the groundwork for what we eventually launched as InsightLens, and the team she helped build continues to honor her legacy of rigorous engineering.

We didn't slow down. In early 2022, we closed the acquisition of DataCraft. Integrating the DataCraft team was a turning point for our culture and our stack. It brought in the technical expertise we needed to eventually transition from Kafka to Apache Pulsar, a move that solidified our platform's reliability. That acquisition also paved the way for us to sign the Phoenix Corp MSA—a landmark enterprise deal with demanding reliability requirements that pushed our engineering bar higher.

Our engineering team has been the backbone of every milestone. I am particularly proud of how the team handled "Project Phoenix"—the massive Python 2 to 3 migration. It was a Herculean effort that allowed us to keep scaling as we launched PulseConnect.

As we moved into 2023, the leadership structure evolved alongside the product. Sarah Chen, who had been leading our engineering organization since the early days, stepped into the CTO role. Under her guidance, we split the organization into Platform Engineering and Product Engineering, a move that allowed us to stay agile as the company scaled significantly.

Even when things go wrong—like the NexusFlow outage in August 2023—we rely on our transparency. The Data Platform team showed exactly why we have such a talented group of people, working through the rate-limiter configuration issues to get us back online.

Today, with our Series C funding secured, Vertexia stands as a leader in the data space. Whether it's NexusFlow, InsightLens, or PulseConnect, we are still doing exactly what Diana and I set out to do in that San Francisco basement: making data work for everyone.

— Arjun Mehta, CEO & Co-founder, Vertexia Inc.
